## Descriptions

### Jakob (Character)
A boy of about ten, narrow through the shoulders, in hand-me-down denim overalls a size too big with the cuffs rolled twice. Home-cut dark hair, uneven over the ears. Sunburnt neck; dust worked into the creases of his knuckles and the seams of his boots. He watches more than he speaks, and he has the particular stillness of a child who has learned that questions have a cost. 1930s Dust Bowl, muted ochre and bone-grey palette, hard low sunlight.

### Father (Character)
A lean, close-shaved man in his mid-forties, tall enough to stoop in his own doorways. Collarless work shirt buttoned to the throat, sleeves rolled to the elbow, forearms roped from field work. Sandy hair greying at the temples, deep-set pale eyes, a mouth that rests in something close to kindness. Nothing about him reads as a threat, and that is the point: his warmth is genuine rather than a mask, which is what makes what he is building unbearable. He is the reference face for the Store Clerk, the Town Constable and the Sitting Woman — generate him first and reuse him. If you want his arc on his face, regenerate per chapter: rested and steady at Errand 1, sleepless and glassy by Errand 3, gold-lit and hollowed out at Errand 5.

### Tobin (Character)
An enormous man in a rubber butcher's apron over a canvas smock, both gone dark and stiff with old work. Hands and forearms of someone who lifts carcasses for a living. His face stays under the brim of a low cap or just outside the frame — never render him fully; he is far worse as a shape and a smell than as a face. He is not malicious and never hurries. He handles Jakob the way you would handle livestock that isn't ready yet: without cruelty, and without the slightest doubt that he will be back for it.

### Night Crusher (Character)
Not a figure but an absence roughly the size of a man — a silhouette that reads as a hole punched through the image, edges unresolved, film grain and static bleeding where the outline should be. No face, no hands, no clothing, no light on it. It never chases and never speaks. It arrives after the failure, gathers Jakob up almost tenderly, and puts him back where he started. That is the horror of it: it is not the punishment, it is the mechanism that makes the punishment repeatable.

### Grandfather Wieland (Character)
A very old man grown into a wall of packed soil and white root — ribs, legs and one shoulder already earth, with only the face and a single free hand still clear of it. Root threads run beneath the skin like veins. His eyes are open, calm, and entirely alive; he has been down here long enough to have stopped minding. Lit only by a low flame burning in a stone cup, the colour of a held breath.

### Store Clerk (Character)
This is Father's face, exactly — same bone structure, same age, same pale deep-set eyes. Generate from the Father reference and change only the dressing: shopkeeper's collar, sleeve garters, hair parted on the opposite side. The scene works on recognition, so any drift in the face breaks it. He stands behind a counter of dust-furred canned goods stacked in perfect pyramids, every label faced out, arranged a long time ago and never sold from.

### Town Constable (Character)
Father's face again, from the same reference — but weathered a decade further, or a decade less. The ambiguity is deliberate; Jakob cannot decide which, and neither should the viewer. Iron star badge, dark flat-brimmed hat, high buttoned collar. He stands where the street stops being a street, and he places himself between Jakob and the road out of town without ever appearing to move.

### Sitting Woman (Character)
Father's bone structure beneath a woman's face and hair — the same jaw, the same deep-set eyes, unmistakable once noticed. Faded print dress, dust to the knee, hands open and slack in her lap. She sits on the lip of a dry well and does not look up. Flies walk on her forearm and she lets them. The well stone under her palms has been worn into two smooth cups.

### Narrator (Character)
Voice only — no portrait; leave this asset without an image. Dry, unhurried, and specific about physical detail: temperature, smell, the sound a particular room makes. It never explains the supernatural and never reassures. Its one tell is that it occasionally knows things Jakob has not been told, and states them flatly, one beat too late to be of any use to him.
